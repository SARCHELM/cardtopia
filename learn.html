Learn to Play 5 Card Games

Hearts

Game Type
Hearts is a trick-taking card game where each player competes individually.

Age & Players
Typically recommended for ages 13 and up. It’s most often played with four players, though variations accommodate three or five.

The Pack
The game uses a standard 52-card deck.

Object of the Game
Your goal is to finish with the lowest total score. The game ends once a player reaches or exceeds a pre-agreed point threshold (commonly 100).

Card Values / Scoring
At the end of each round, you tally one point for each heart you’ve taken and 13 points for the queen of spades. If a player captures all 13 hearts and the queen of spades in one hand, they score zero while each opponent adds 26 points.



The Deal
Cards are dealt face-down, one at a time, clockwise. In a four-player game, each gets 13 cards. For three players, remove the 2 of diamonds and deal 17 cards each. In five-player play, remove the 2 of clubs so each player can get 10 cards.

The Play
The player holding the 2 of clubs starts. (If that card was removed, then the 3 of clubs leads.) Players must follow the suit led if able; if not, they may play any other card—but they may not discard a heart or the queen of spades in the first trick unless they have no clubs. The highest card of the suit led wins the trick, and the winner leads the next. Hearts cannot be led until a heart or the queen of spades has been played (“broken”). There is no trump suit.





Rummy 500

Game Type
Rummy 500 is a classic card game focused on matching sets and runs, combining strategy and luck.

Age & Players
Recommended for ages 8 and up. 2 to 8 players can participate. With 2 players, each gets 13 cards; with 3 or more players, each gets 7 cards.

The Pack
The game uses a standard 52-card deck.

Object of the Game
The aim is to be the first player to reach 500 net points by forming melds (sets of 3 or 4 of a kind) and/or sequences (runs of 3 or more cards in the same suit), and by being the first to empty your hand.

Card Values / Scoring
Points are scored by laying down sets or runs, and by adding cards to existing melds. Cards between 2-9 are worth 5 points. Cards between 10, J, Q, and K are worth 10 points. If Aces are played as 1 (ex: A, 2, 3), then it is worth 5 points. If Aces are played as 13 or a set, then it is worth 15 points. When a player empties their hand, the round ends. Each player’s score is calculated as:

  • Points for all cards they have laid on the table.
  • Minus points for all cards still in their hand.



The Deal
With 3 or more players: each is dealt 7 cards. With 2 players: each receives 13 cards. The remaining deck forms the stock, placed face‑down in the center. Turn the top card face‑up beside it to begin the discard pile.

The Play
Play starts with the player to the dealer’s left and moves clockwise. On a turn, a player may draw:

  • The top card of the stock, or
  • Any card from the discard pile — if drawing from the discard pile, you must also take all cards lying on top of it. The chosen card must be immediately used in a meld or added to an existing meld. The extra cards may be melded immediately or kept for future turns.

After drawing (and before discarding), a player may lay down any sets or sequences and/or add cards to melds already on the table. Note: Runs cannot wrap around (A‑K‑Q and A‑2‑3 are okay; K‑A‑2 is not allowed).





BS

Game Type
A fast‑paced, bluff‑style card game where players attempt to shed their cards while deceiving opponents and calling out their bluffs.

Age & Players
Generally suitable for older children, teens and adults (for example, age 8+), since it involves bluffing and strategic risk‑taking. Requires 3 or more players.

The Pack
The game uses a standard 52-card deck.

Object of the Game
The goal is to be the first player to get rid of all the cards in your hand. To do so you must play cards in sequence and may lie about what you are playing; your opponents may challenge you.

Card Values / Scoring
Cards are played by rank in a set sequence: Ace, 2, 3, 4, … up to King, then back to Ace. There is no numeric “scoring” system in the usual sense (points); success is simply dropping all your cards first. The risk: if you are challenged and caught bluffing, you must pick up the entire discard pile. If you are challenged and found to be honest, the challenger picks up the pile.



The Deal
Shuffle the deck, deal all the cards out to the players (some may have one card more than another). Jokers are excluded in the standard version. Once dealt, players organize their hands and a starting player is chosen.

The Play

  1. The starting player is chosen (commonly the one holding the Ace of Spades, or by another agreed method).
  2. The starting player places one or more cards face down and claims them to be the required rank (for example, “one Ace” or “two Aces”). If they don’t actually have that rank, they must bluff.
  3. Play proceeds clockwise; each next player must claim and place the next rank in sequence (for example if the previous claim was Aces, the next player claims Twos, then Threes, etc.).
  4. At any time a player may challenge (“BS!”) the previous player’s claim before the next player plays. If the challenged player’s cards are revealed and were not as claimed, the bluffer picks up the discard pile; if they were truthful, the challenger picks up the pile.
  5. After a challenge resolution, play continues with the next rank and next player in sequence. The objective remains to empty your hand.
  6. The game ends when a player plays their last card(s), provided they are not challenged successfully before the next turn starts. That player wins.




Capitalism

Game Type
A social shedding‑type card game where players aim to be the first to get rid of all their cards, jockeying for the best role (President) and avoiding the worst (Scum). You choose to have a Vice-President and Vice-Scum if there are more than 4 people.

Age & Players
Recommended for a general audience (teenagers and adults). Generally for 4 to 7 players.

The Pack
The game uses a standard 52-card deck.

Object of the Game
The goal is to be the first player to discard all your cards in a round. The first to do so becomes the “President,” the second becomes “Vice President,” and the last with cards becomes the “Scum," the second becomes Vice Scum." Future rounds carry role/status consequences.

Card Values / Scoring

  • Card ranks from highest to lowest: Ace (high), King, Queen, Jack, 10, 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3.
  • The 2’s are special: playing a 2 clears the current pile and the player who plays it starts the next round of plays. You can NOT end the game with a 2. Doing so, will result in the player becoming Scum automatically.
  • Scoring (for each round): the President earns 2 points; the Vice President earns 1 point. The first player to reach 11 points wins the game.
  • Role‑based card exchange: After the round, the Scum must give 2 cards to the President that they requested. The President then trades 2 cards they choose. Same goes for the Vice-President and Vice-Scum, but only 1 card is traded.


The Deal
Cards are dealt face-down, one at a time, clockwise. After the first game, cards are to be dealt into piles, and then the top cards are flipped face-up. If there are duplicate cards, then flip the 2nd card, below the duplicates, face up. President then gets to choose which pile they desire, then the Vice-President, and so forth, based on the order of which players threw all of their cards from the previous game. Scum will of course pick last.

The Play

  1. The first round begins with the player holding the 3 of clubs (or as agreed) playing it to start the pile.
  2. Play moves clockwise. On a turn, a player must either pass or play cards that beat the previous play (matching the count/type and of higher rank).
  3. If a player cannot or chooses not to beat the previous play, they pass. When all players in turn pass (and the play returns to the last person who played something), the pile clears and the last player to play starts a new pile.
  4. Playing a 2 clears the current pile immediately; the player who plays the 2 starts a new pile. A player cannot play a 2 as their last card; they automatically become the Scum for the next round.
  5. Special rule: If someone plays a four‑of‑a‑kind aka completion (all four cards of a rank) whenever possible—even out of turn—the pile clears and that player starts the next pile.
  6. After each round, exchanges will happen. The game continues until one player accumulates 11 total points and is declared the winner.




Speed

Game Type
A fast‑paced, two‑player card game where each player tries to quickly shed their cards into central piles without taking turns.

Age & Players
Suitable for around ages 8 and up, since it depends on reaction speed and card‑sequencing rather than complex rules. Typically played by 2 players, though variations allow more with extra decks.

The Pack
The game uses a standard 52-card deck.

Object of the Game
The goal is to be the first player to play all of your cards into the central piles — when you empty your hand (and your stack) you shout “Speed!” and you win.

Card Values / Scoring

  • Cards follow the usual ranks: Ace, 2, 3, … up to King.
  • The ranks “wrap around”: for example a King may be followed by an Ace, and an Ace by a 2.
  • There is no point‑based scoring system in the classic version—winning is simply being first to shed all your cards.


The Deal

  1. Deal each player a fixed number of cards (for two players, often 20 each).
  2. Place two “draw” piles or replacement piles (commonly sets of five cards each) on each side of the central area.
  3. Then flip two cards face‑up in the centre between the players to begin the game.

The Play

  • Both players play simultaneously (no turn‑taking).
  • On the central two face‑up cards, players may place cards from their hand that are one rank higher or one rank lower than the card. Because of the wrap‑around rule, a King can go to Ace and a 2 can go to Ace in some variations.
  • After playing a card from their hand, the player draws another card from their personal draw pile so that their hand returns to a set number (commonly 5 cards) until the draw pile is exhausted.
  • If neither player can play a card (i.e., both are “stuck”), each simultaneously flips a card from their side‑draw piles (or replacement piles) onto the two centre piles and play resumes.
  • If even after flipping the side piles no play is possible, those piles may be turned over and reused (according to variation) and play continues.
  • The game ends when a player has no more cards in hand and no more cards in their draw deck (depending on setup). That player wins and typically shouts “Speed!” to signal victory.
