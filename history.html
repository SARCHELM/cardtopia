History of Cards

Timeline of Playing Cards

9th C.
Origins traced to China during the Tang dynasty; early forms of card‑like games emerge.
1377
Earliest major European reference to playing cards in a Swiss monastery manuscript.
15th C.
French standardize the modern suits: hearts, spades, diamonds, clubs; red/black system begins.
1828
England mandates the Ace of Spades tax stamp to prevent card‑manufacturing tax evasion.
1860s
Mass production expands; companies like De La Rue help standardize card printing.
1875
Corner indices patented by NY Consolidated Card Co., forming the modern card corner ranks.

The modern deck is the result of a long international journey: from early card-like games in Asia, through Italy/Spain, Germany, France, England and finally to the U.S. Each region added key innovations: suits, court structure, printing methods, standardized design and extra cards like the Joker. What we hold in our hands today — a 52-card deck with four suits, two colours, court cards and Jokers — owes its shape to hundreds of years of cultural exchange and technological development.

The East

The true origin of playing cards is uncertain; they likely emerged in Asia (China, India, Korea, Persia or Egypt) and spread west. Cards from the 9th century in China may have represented coins and gambling stakes, and similar iconography shows up later in Europe. One possible route: from the Mamluk period in Egypt into Europe, where early decks had suits like cups, coins, swords, and polo-sticks (paralleling later Italian Latin suits).

Italy and Spain

The first confirmed European references to playing cards appear in Italy and Spain in the 14th and 15th centuries. Italian/Spanish “Latin suits”: swords, clubs, cups, coins. In Spain especially, early decks lacked certain numbers (no 8s/9s) and used a 40-card deck for games like ombre. Italian early decks: hand-painted, luxury items. As production improved, cards became more widely available.

Germany

Germany introduced its own suit set: acorns, leaves, hearts and bells — reflecting everyday and rural life. Court card structure shifted: kings and two knaves (obermann and untermann) instead of queen. Printing innovations in Germany (wood-cutting, engraving) enabled mass production and Germany became a major exporter of playing cards.

France

The French in the 15th century created the familiar suits we use today: hearts, spades, diamonds, clubs (coeurs, piques, carreaux, trefles). They also introduced the red-and-black colour scheme (two red suits, two black suits), enabling faster, cheaper stencil printing. Court cards gained named identities (Kings: David, Alexander the Great, Charlemagne, Julius Caesar; Queens: Athena, Judith, Rachel, Argine) in late 1500s France.

England and United States

England adopted the French suit symbols but used the English names: hearts, spades, diamonds, clubs. The iconic status of the Ace of Spades comes from English tax laws: by 1828 the Ace of Spades had to bear proof of tax payment, which made it ornate and special. In the 1800s, English innovations (especially by printers like Thomas de la Rue) standardized card design, introduced double-ended court cards (so players didn’t reveal face cards when holding the hand), and made decks more uniform. The U.S. was later to become a major producer of cards, initially importing English decks. Some American makers even labelled their Ace of Spades with “London” for prestige. Important innovations: corner indices (introduced in the U.S. by the New York Consolidated Card Company in the 1870s) to help card recognition. The American addition of the Joker (originating in mid-19th century U.S., derived from the game euchre) became standard: first recorded use ~1875. Today, U.S. companies (especially the United States Playing Card Company) dominate production of the standard 52-card deck with two Jokers.
